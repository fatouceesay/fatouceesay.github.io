What is race?

Race is the classification of human beings into categories based on physical characteristics. There are many different views of race, but the two main views are the Social Construct View and the Scientific view:

  • Social Construct View: The idea of race is a product of human choices and history rather than something rooted primarily in nature, genetics, or biology. We invent racial categories, we apply those categories to people and there are social implications to belonging in those categories. When people are assigned to such categories, it is the called the act of socialization, and over time, these sociological decisions and actions may seem like they were always there – as given or natural – rather than social constructions.
  • Scientific View: During the mid-19th century, when the Enlightenment was in full bloom, many otherwise ambiguous theories were challenged by the scientific idea. One of these theories included race, which was now e explained by the science of genetics. The scientific view claimed that because the differences were biological, they were bound to be permanent and persistent. Race was assigned based on arbitrary characteristics such as skin color. Though the viewpoint has been largely discredited, it has not disappeared. In 1994, the book the Bell Curve: Intelligence and Class Structure in American Life made controversial claims that black people were inherently less intelligent than white people.

Who does race affect?

Race affects everyone. In the United States, every person is first categorized by their race. When applying for jobs, Identification, even when applying to college, all applications require you provide your race. Although it is largely accepted that race is a social construct, and therefore not real, it is real in the consequences towards People of Color (POC). POC have always been treated differently because of their physical or cultural differences. Even in today's society, there is discrimination of minorities all across the nation. Many times white people attribute this to the behaviors of the victims, and fail to realize that they hold a certain privilege that POC can never have.

Definitions

  • Race: The categorization of humans based on physical traits

  • Racism: Prejudice or discrimination of an entire race based on the belief that their own race is superior.

  • Equality: The state of being equal, especially in status, rights, and opportunities.

  • Equity: The quality of being fair and impartial.

  • Ally: Someone who makes the commitment and effort to recognize their privilege (based on gender, class, race, sexual identity, etc.) and work in solidarity with oppressed groups in the struggle for justice.

  • Bigotry: Intolerant prejudice that glorifies one's own group and denigrates members of other groups.

  • Culture: A social system of meaning and customs that are developed by a group of people to assure its adaptation and survival. These groups are distinguished by a set of unspoken rules that shape values, beliefs, habits, patterns of thinking, behaviors and styles of communication.

  • Ethnicity: A social construct that divides people into smaller social groups based on characteristics such as shared sense of group membership, values, behavioral patterns, language, political and economic interests, history and ancestral geographical base.

  • Diversity: Includes all the ways in which people differ, and it encompasses all the different characteristics that make one individual or group different from another. It is all-inclusive and recognizes everyone and every group as part of the diversity that should be valued.

  • Oppression: Systemic devaluing, undermining, marginalizing, and disadvantaging of certain social identities in contrast to the privileged norm; when some people are denied something of value, while others have ready access.

  • Power: Power is unequally distributed globally and in U.S. society; some individuals or groups wield greater power than others, thereby allowing them greater access and control over resources. Wealth, whiteness, citizenship, patriarchy, heterosexism, and education are a few key social mechanisms through which power operates.

  • White Supremacy: White supremacy is a historically based, institutionally perpetuated system of exploitation and oppression of continents, nations and peoples of color by white peoples and nations of the European continent; for the purpose of maintaining and defending a system of wealth, power and privilege.

  • Inclusion: Authentically bringing traditionally excluded individuals and/or groups into processes, activities, and decision/policy making in a way that shares power.

  • Intersectionality: Exposing [one’s] multiple identities can help clarify they ways in which a person can simultaneously experience privilege and oppression. For example, a black man is more privileged than a black woman in the U.S.

  • White Privilege: Refers to the unquestioned and unearned set of advantages, entitlements, benefits and choices bestowed on people solely because they are white. Generally white people who experience such privilege do so without being conscious of it. A system of white domination that creates and maintains belief systems that make current racial advantages and disadvantages seem normal. The system includes powerful incentives for maintaining white privilege and its consequences, and powerful negative consequences for trying to interrupt white privilege or reduce its consequences in meaningful ways.

  • Privilege: Unearned social power accorded by the formal and informal institutions of society to ALL members of a dominant group (e.g. white privilege, male privilege, etc.). Privilege is usually invisible to those who have it because we’re taught not to see it, but nevertheless it puts them at an advantage over those who do not have it.
